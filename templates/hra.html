    Navigating HR Challenges in FPO Ecosystem

    While most literature on FPOs concentrates on finance, marketing, and infrastructure, the human resource dimension—who leads the organization, how professionals are recruited and retained, and how capacity is built—has received relatively little emphasis. That’s one of the greatest gaps in these efforts — particularly since the right leadership and staffing translate financial and policy support into meaningful outcomes. Most FPOs operate with low member equity and inadequate working capital, making it difficult to recruit and retain appropriate employees. According to NABARD studies, more than 60 percent of FPOs do not have full-time CEOs or professionals, so leadership is in the hands of farmer-directors who, while committed, may lack the managerial expertise needed to navigate compliance, market negotiations, and growth strategies. HR systems are affected directly by financial constraints. Because many organizations cannot afford competitive salaries or sustained training programs, they must rely on temporary, project-based staff. It also detracts from continuity, preventing the establishment of professional cultures within an organization. Marketing challenges compound the problem. Farmers often lack access to real-time price information, storage facilities, and reliable buyers. The lack of market information is a common limiting factor cited by study after study, including by ICAR and NABARD, as one of the most frequent problems of FPOs. In that context, the requirement for professional marketing managers that negotiate contracts, and accept build value chains, and secure buyers is essential. However, with limited funds, most FPOs are forced to depend on directors or local leaders with limited exposure to formal markets, which diminishes their leverage to drive negotiation and sustainability of competitive advantage.

    HR in FPOs is additionally complicated by policy and institutional arrangements. Although government support has been substantial — from equity grants to credit guarantee schemes — the regulatory environment is complicated. The registration requirements differ by state, compliance standards are heavy, and scheme-based support is often temporary. For the FPO leaders, a lot of their energy goes to paperwork and compliance rather than business development. The temporary nature of government schemes also undermines job security for professionals, making it difficult to retain skilled staff in rural areas where alternative career opportunities are scarce.

    Socio-cultural factors add yet more complexity. FPOs do not exist as economic aggregates, but rather as integrated systems of local communities in which hierarchies, norms, and cultural dynamics influence decision-making. Women, for instance, are a considerable proportion of the agricultural workforce yet underrepresented in leadership at most FPOs. FAO reports underscore the importance of women in agricultural collectives, but their participation in governance often remains symbolic. When professional managers are hired from outside the local community, there is also the issue of mistrust, creating tensions between participatory governance and professional expertise. Low literacy levels combined with poor digital skills and overreliance on local customs also shape HR decisions and how organizational roles are implemented.

    Robust research approaches are required to understand HR in FPOs, that incorporate both numbers and narratives. The best way forward is through a mixed-methods approach — quantitative surveys and qualitative interviews. Having geographic or commodity based stratified sampling ensures representation, while survey tools can ascertain how staffing levels link to performance measures like turnover or member satisfaction. Statistical methods, for example Garrett’s ranking method, enable constraints prioritization, or regression analysis to evaluate the association of HR investments (e.g., training hours, staff retention) to organizational outcomes (e.g., revenue per member). Simultaneously, semi-structured interviews can illuminate issues of trust, leadership, gender inclusion and local governance that are often hidden from the numerical datasets. Such methodological rigor guarantees that HR challenges are not anecdotal, but are systematically understood.

    Findings from existing evaluations support these challenges with empirical evidence. They stress the severity of the situation. The country has over 42,000 registered FPOs; but studies show only ten to 15 percent have achieved financial sustainability. Staffing is one of the weakest areas, with most organizations lacking permanent CEOs or professionals. While financial and marketing restrictions dominate, governance and socio-cultural barriers significantly shape the usage and management of human capital as well. These insights highlight that HR cannot be handled in a vacuum; it is interwoven with every other aspect of the ecosystem.

    For FPOs to better their HR department, a multi-part strategy is necessary. Innovative paid forms of compensation can combine modest pay with performance-based incentives, which can include profit-sharing or margins on procurement, thus making jobs financially attractive even under tight budgets. Fellow FPOs at nearby firms also might want to try a concept of shared work, or employ accountants, marketing people, or technical specialists, in partnership together--for which their costs decrease but still maintain professionalism. Capacity building actions have to be accompanied by accountability measures so that there can be assessment of training programs based on measurable outcomes such as greater price realization or better member participation. Digital HR systems, which are based on basic mobile apps, are convenient yet can make attendance, maintenance and monitoring easier, and without imposing undue administrative overhead. Gender-sensitive policies, such as quotas for female leaders, flexible labor options or programmatic digital literacy initiatives, inclusivity and organizational inclusiveness, can be enhanced and institutional legitimacy enhanced. Finally, policy advocacy is strong. If governments can subsidize infrastructure and credit, HR can also be supported directly with wage subsidies, HR development grants, or rural fellowship programs bringing trained professionals to the sector for longer terms.
